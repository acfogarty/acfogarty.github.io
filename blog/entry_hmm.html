Resources for implementing a HMM from scratch

Reference values for debugging May 25, 2018

There exist plenty of off-the-shelf Hidden Markov Model packages such as HMMLearn (previously part of scikit-learn), but if you want to implement one from scratch to understand how it works, you'll be coding three algorithms:

  • forward part of the forward-backward algorithm - given the transition and emission probabilities and a sequence of observations, get the probability that the given model generated the given sequence
  • Baum-Welch algorithm (basically expectation-maximization) - given a sequence of observations, fit the transition and emission probabilities
  • Viterbi algorithm - given the transition and emission probabilities and a sequence of observations, get the most likely sequence of hidden states

Two great articles that clearly illustrate the Baum-Welch algorithm are:

Hidden Markov Models and You, Part Two, Society of Actuaries, Forecasting and Futurism, Dec 2013

Goes through the Baum-Welch algorithm step-by-step, giving example calculations for each individual part of the expectation and maximization steps. Perfect for use as reference values for debugging.

An Interactive Spreadsheet for Teaching the Forward-Backward Algorithm by Jason Eisner

This is the origin of the popular Baltimore ice cream example (observations = number of ice creams sold, hidden states = hot or cold weather). Actually I didn't use it, but it looks pretty detailed, and there's an Excel spreadsheet with example calculations

For the Viterbi algorithm, some sample data one can use for testing/debugging:

Ivan Kutskir's blog

Blackarbs blog

(Viterbi algorithm is not the only possibility here, just the most common one.)

Finally of course I have to mention the classic HMM tutorial paper from the 80s

A Tutorial on Hidden Markov Models and Selected Applications in Speech Recognition by Lawrence Rabiner

Extremely detailed and yet very readable. Also includes all the practical implementation details that simplistic tutorials leave out (scaling to avoid terms going to zero, choice of initial estimates, effects of insufficient training data).
